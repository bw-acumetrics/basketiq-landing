# BasketIQ Market Intelligence
## Botswana Soft Drinks Snapshot — Q1 2026
### Category Intelligence Report | Gaborone Metropolitan Area

---

## Executive Summary

This report examines **599 classified soft drinks purchases** captured across **500 verified baskets** from **168 Gaborone consumers** between **February and April 2026**, collected via BasketIQ's WhatsApp-based receipt panel.

**The headline finding:** one company — The Coca-Cola Company — is present on roughly **seven in every ten** soft drinks purchased in our Gaborone sample. Coke alone accounts for **43%** of all units and **more than six in every ten Pula** spent on the top five brands. The rest of the category is split thinly between a small number of independents, a handful of PepsiCo brands, and a long tail of local labels.

For Botswana brands competing outside the Coca-Cola family, this is a concentration problem worth understanding in detail. For Coca-Cola itself, it is confirmation that category leadership in this market is neither abstract nor contested at the top — it is total.

*Source: BasketIQ receipt panel, Gaborone, Feb 1 – Apr 13 2026 | N=599 soft drinks line items across 500 baskets, 168 consumers.*

## 1. Soft drinks appear in roughly one in every seven Gaborone baskets.

Of every verified basket in our Q1 2026 Gaborone panel, **13.8%** contained at least one soft drinks line item. When soft drinks are in the basket, the average spend on the category alone is **BWP 26.89** — a single large bottle or two small ones. Soft drinks are not an everyday staple in the local basket the way bread and milk are, but they are a consistent fixture of roughly one shop in seven.

| Distinct baskets containing soft drinks | 500 |

## 2. One company owns seven in every ten soft drinks purchases.

The single most striking feature of the category in Botswana is not competition — it is consolidation. Grouping brands by parent company, The Coca-Cola Company accounts for **70.8%** of every unit sold in the sample. PepsiCo sits at roughly **5%**. All remaining brands combined — independents, regional entrants, and smaller local labels — share the remaining **24%**.

| Parent company | Brands in sample | Units | Share of category |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The Coca-Cola Company** | Coke, Fanta, Sprite, Sparletta, Schweppes, Stoney | **424** | **70.8%** |
| All independents and local brands | Refresh, Twizza, and others | 144 | 24.0% |
| PepsiCo | Pepsi, Mirinda, 7Up | 31 | 5.2% |
| **Total** | | **599** | **100%** |

There is no other FMCG category in our dataset where a single corporate owner comes close to this level of dominance. For any brand manager building a soft drinks strategy for Botswana, the competitive set is not "other soft drinks" — it is *one incumbent, five distant challengers, and everyone else.*

*Source: BasketIQ receipt panel, Feb 1 – Apr 13 2026 | N=599 soft drinks line items | Parent-company attribution based on publicly disclosed brand ownership in Southern Africa as of 2026. Any misattribution should be flagged to hello@basketiq.co.bw.*

## 3. Coca-Cola, on its own, is the most-purchased single brand by a factor of three.

Zooming in from parent company to individual brand, **Coca-Cola (Coke)** alone captures **43.4%** of every soft drinks unit sold in the sample — more than **three times** the share of the second-place brand. The next four brands together barely match Coke's volume on their own.

Notably, the two largest non-Coca-Cola challengers are both regional independents rather than PepsiCo labels. Pepsi itself appears in only **23** baskets across the full Q1 period — a share smaller than Sparletta, Sprite, or even the sixth-place Schweppes.

## 4. Coca-Cola captures more than six in every ten Pula spent on the top five brands.

By revenue the picture is even more skewed than by units. Across the five best-selling brands in Botswana, Coca-Cola alone accounts for **61.6%** of category Pula. When Fanta and Sparletta are added — also Coca-Cola Company brands — that rises to **82.0%** of the top-five pool. The two largest independent challengers, Refresh and Twizza, together capture just **18.0%** of the same revenue pool.

| Brand | Revenue (BWP) | Share of top-5 revenue |
|---|---|---|
| **Coke** | 6,221.30 | **61.6%** |
| Fanta | 1,349.19 | 13.4% |
| Refresh | 944.11 | 9.3% |
| Twizza | 874.26 | 8.7% |
| Sparletta | 713.91 | 7.1% |
| **Top-5 total** | **10,102.77** | **100%** |

The gap between Coke and the next four brands is not a unit-price story alone — Coca-Cola is also the category's most frequently purchased SKU. Volume and revenue leadership reinforce each other.

*Source: BasketIQ receipt panel, Feb 1 – Apr 13 2026 | Revenue calculated as the sum of line-item totals (BWP) for each brand | Top 5 brands by unit volume shown.*

## 5. Most soft drinks purchases cost between P10 and P15.

Almost **six in every ten** soft drinks purchases in the Q1 panel are below **P15** per line item — the price range corresponding to single-serve bottles and cans. The modal price band is **P10 – P15**, capturing more than a third of all purchases. Only about **one in eight** line items exceeds **P30**, typically multi-litre bottles or multipacks.

| Price band (BWP) | Line items | Share of purchases |
|---|---|---|
| Under P5 | 18 | 3.0% |
| P5 – P10 | 125 | 21.0% |
| **P10 – P15** | **206** | **34.7%** |
| P15 – P20 | 80 | 13.5% |
| P20 – P30 | 86 | 14.5% |
| Over P30 | 79 | 13.3% |
| **Total** | **594** | **100%** |

Two takeaways for brand managers. First, the category is **price-anchored below P15** — any brand positioning above that line is targeting a narrower 41% of purchase occasions, not the mass market. Second, the long tail above **P30** is thinner than it looks in revenue terms: fewer than one in seven purchases, but those purchases carry most of the incremental Pula.

*Source: BasketIQ receipt panel, Feb 1 – Apr 13 2026 | N=594 soft drinks line items with a recorded line total | Price bands computed from line-item total BWP, not unit price.*

## 6. When soft drinks are in the basket, bread and milk are along for the ride.

Soft drinks rarely travel alone. Of every basket that contained a soft drink in the Q1 panel, the most common companions were everyday staples and snacking categories — bread, fresh milk, and chips — rather than other beverages. The top co-purchased category across soft-drinks baskets was **bread**, appearing in **65** of the 500 baskets.

| Co-purchased category | Baskets sharing with soft drinks | Share of soft-drinks baskets |
|---|---|---|
| Bread | 65 | 13.0% |
| Fresh milk | 63 | 12.6% |
| Chips and snacks | 62 | 12.4% |
| Juices (other beverages) | 43 | 8.6% |

The soft-drink-plus-snack basket (soft drinks + chips) is nearly as common as soft-drinks-plus-bread. For retailers and suppliers alike, this points to two distinct missions in the same category: a **top-up grocery shop** where a soft drink is added to a staples run, and a **convenience / snacking occasion** where soft drinks pair with salty snacks.

*Source: BasketIQ receipt panel, Feb 1 – Apr 13 2026 | N=500 baskets containing at least one soft drinks line item | A "co-purchased category" is any product category other than soft drinks appearing in the same basket on the same transaction.*

## What this means for brands

**1. In Botswana, the competitive frame is not "soft drinks" — it is "Coca-Cola vs. the rest."** Any brand plan written against a generic competitive set will underweight the concentration of the market. The relevant question for challenger brands is not "how do I take share from the category," but "where, exactly, is the 29% of the category that Coca-Cola does *not* currently own?"

**2. Price positioning below P15 is where volume lives.** The modal soft drinks purchase in Gaborone is a P10 – P15 single-serve. Premium positioning is viable but narrow — it addresses at most four in ten occasions, and those occasions are disproportionately the larger, multi-litre, share-basket purchases rather than impulse.

**3. Soft drinks are a basket-add, not a basket-driver.** Shoppers in the Q1 panel buy soft drinks alongside bread, milk, and chips — not in isolation. In-store placement, bundling, and promotional timing that align with those staples are the highest-leverage activation points.

## Methodology

- **Data source:** BasketIQ WhatsApp consumer panel, Gaborone metropolitan area
- **Period:** February 1 – April 13, 2026
- **Sample:** 599 soft drinks line items, 500 baskets, 168 distinct consumers
- **Category definition:** Items classified under `BEV_SOFT_DRINKS` in BasketIQ's product catalog. Juices, cordials, bottled water, and energy drinks are tracked as separate categories and excluded from all numbers in this report.
- **Retailer data:** Retailer-level splits, pricing comparisons, and store-specific performance are deliberately excluded from this public report. They are available to subscribers under a separate commercial tier.
- **Parent-company attribution:** Brand-to-parent mappings are based on publicly disclosed ownership in Southern Africa as of 2026. Corrections welcome at hello@basketiq.co.bw.
- **Limitations:** The panel skews toward WhatsApp-active consumers in urban Gaborone, and sample sizes for individual brands outside the top five are small. Treat brand-level comparisons outside the top ten as directional. Classification coverage varies across the product catalog, and the category penetration figure should be read as a lower bound.
